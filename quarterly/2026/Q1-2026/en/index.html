“You can’t see the forest for the trees.” — John Heywood, 1546

We frequently open our letters with quotes from great thinkers — not by habit, but because they capture something enduring about human nature: the tendency to be absorbed by immediate events at the expense of the broader picture. Financial markets are no exception — they amplify this bias, making individual trees far more visible than the forest itself.

Deciphering signals, understanding the full implications of an event without being blinded by its immediacy: this is an arduous, often thankless task. It is tempting, out of simplicity or reflex, to focus on the tree alone. Many trees obscured this quarter's forest: the immediate effects of the Iran war, a challenge to the private credit model in the United States, and potentially structural disruptions born from artificial intelligence.

And yet, it is the state of the forest that matters to us.

From Venezuela to Iran

The American-Israeli coalition's attack on Iran is consistent with Trump's now well-established operating style: brutal and unpredictable. Nine months after B2 Spirit airstrikes on Iranian nuclear facilities, the final blow against the regime appears to have been launched in early March, destabilising not only the region but financial markets as well.

The strategic importance of the Strait of Hormuz — controlled by Iran — implies a reading far beyond any stated objective of world peace, which does not appear to be a strategic priority for the current US administration. It is worth recalling that the United States is energy independent, even more so since Venezuela's vast reserves came under effective US influence following Maduro's removal. A disruption to Hormuz flows increases other nations' dependency on the US — the same logic applies to key commodities such as ammonia and nitrogen, critical inputs in fertilisers and ultimately food production.

The timing of Operation Epic Fury conflicts with Trump's short-term political agenda (oil-driven inflation, falling approval ratings ahead of the midterms), yet aligns perfectly with his broader doctrine: Make America Great Again — asserting global leverage on American terms.

Market Impact

US long-term yields bore the brunt of this instability, rising 50 bps to a high of 4.49% in March — inflation expectations mechanically repriced upward as war costs weigh on public finances, with levels remaining above the Fed's 2% target since mid-2024.

Markets now anticipate a more patient Fed on rate cuts. Powell reiterated late in the month that rate hikes are an ineffective tool against a supply-side oil shock, with demand compression being the natural corrective mechanism. The worst-case scenario for the US remains a period of rate stability. Notably, the Liberation Day tariffs did not generate the inflationary surge their critics had predicted.

European rates are on a very different trajectory: French 10-year yields are at their highest since 2008, German yields since 2011. Lagarde's recent remarks — not ruling out rate hikes if inflation accelerates — do nothing to ease the continent's already strained fiscal situation.

The Iran war does not fundamentally alter the global balance of power in the near term, provided its duration remains limited. We will monitor the longer-term consequences closely.

The Macro-Micro Divide

US macro data shows no major inflection: growth remains positive though slightly decelerating, unemployment stays low, and inflation remains at an acceptable level. However, some indicators (ISM Services, for instance) leave the door open to a more pronounced slowdown. In a scenario where inflation re-accelerates, stagflation risks — low growth combined with elevated prices — would come into focus. Europe offers a similar picture: anaemic growth, inflation at 2.7%, fragile employment, and a wait-and-see ECB.

EPS revisions are the key differentiator. In the United States, Q1 2026 earnings growth estimates have been revised upward to +13.2% year-on-year since December 31 — marking a sixth consecutive quarter of double-digit earnings growth, driven almost exclusively by Technology and Energy. In Europe, EPS revisions remain negative, weighed down by internationally exposed sectors penalised by euro strength and energy cost pressures.

At NVIDIA's GTC Day in March, Jensen Huang announced a planned doubling of Rubin and Blackwell processor purchases, from $500bn to $1,000bn by 2027. These figures are striking, set against three years of AI hypergrowth. The US growth trajectory remains well-anchored, driven by an unusual combination: ongoing reindustrialisation, growing energy independence, and a technology sector that — while its direct GDP contribution remains modest near-term — is a powerful engine for corporate earnings growth. It is this combination, not a single driver, that gives American economic dynamics their resilience in the face of this quarter's successive shocks.

AI, like any transformative technology, creates value for some actors while beginning to destroy it for others.

AI's First Victims
iShares Expanded Tech-Software ETF (IGV) — ~−35% (TradingView)

Artificial intelligence has begun to produce its first tangible negative effects, most visibly among software publishers: the so-called SaaSpocalypse (a contraction of Software-As-A-Service and Apocalypse). The narrative runs as follows: it is now possible for companies to build their own bespoke software tools in-house, bypassing traditional software vendors and integrators. Listed players have shed over 35% on fears about competitive positioning and pricing power.

The impact is now being felt in private markets, where promises of partial liquidity are being stress-tested. In Q1 2026, several major private credit managers activated redemption limitation mechanisms (“gates”) in the face of an unprecedented wave of withdrawal requests, particularly from vehicles with significant software exposure.

At Blue Owl Capital¹, redemption requests reached a historic level: approximately $5.4bn across two funds, representing 21.9% to 40.7% of AUM depending on the vehicle, including a technology-focused fund with over 30% exposure to software. Facing this surge, the firm authorised only 5% of quarterly redemptions — a satisfaction rate below 25% in some cases.

The same dynamic played out at Apollo Global Management²: its Apollo Debt Solutions vehicle received redemption requests equivalent to approximately 11% of shares outstanding (over $1.5bn), but was only able to honour approximately $730m — less than 50% of amounts requested — applying the standard 5% quarterly cap.

The phenomenon is systemic: in aggregate, major managers (Apollo, KKR, BlackRock, etc.) faced over $10bn in redemption requests in Q1 2026, honouring approximately 70% on average, with significant dispersion across funds depending on sectoral exposure.

Private Credit has grown from $300bn to over $2,000bn in a decade. This structural pressure raises legitimate questions about the sustainability of return promises — we too often forget that reduced liquidity is the price paid. We believe AI is not a passing fad. The SaaSpocalypse looks more like a healthy correction in an overvalued market, a reminder that "trees don't grow to the sky." Our concern is not the survival of SaaS companies, but the contagion effects that could follow.

The "retailisation" trend in private equity and private credit — a decade in the making — is set to be at the epicentre of the storm. Opening 401k accounts to these fund types represents the bridgehead for a massive risk transfer from the shadow banking system to retail investors who are poorly informed about the true risks, blinded by promises of (too) high returns. This is not yet a systemic risk — 401(k)s cannot be used as mortgage collateral, and credit regulation remains strict.

Performance & Positioning
Equities

The equity sleeve posted a limited decline of −2.2% over the quarter, in a context disrupted by the Iran conflict, significantly outperforming benchmark indices: S&P 500 −4.6%, EuroStoxx 50 −4.5%, CAC 40 −4.1%.

Our sector allocation allowed us to limit pure-play AI exposure while maintaining selective technology positions: Space (+17%), Semiconductors (+9.8%) and US Energy (+35%, a position initiated shortly after the Venezuela developments) were the primary shock absorbers this quarter. International tensions and the stronger dollar weighed on China and Europe, with our sectoral diversification within those geographies unable to fully offset the broad-based declines.

With the market correction since the start of the Iran episode remaining contained, we believe a measured re-exposure to equities — with a relative preference for the United States — is warranted, maintaining a balance between defensive and cyclical positioning.

Fixed Income

The fixed income sleeve is deliberately defensive in construction, with an average duration held at 5.51 years and a strong concentration in quality sovereigns — US and European government bonds together representing over 80% of the allocation — reflecting a clear priority on capital preservation in an uncertain rate environment. Performance has been stable and consistent with this risk profile: short and intermediate US maturities (0-1yr +2.5%, 1-3yr +2.8%, 7-10yr +5.1%) and EUR High Yield (+4.5%) contributed positively, partially offsetting the detractors. Long maturities (US 20yr+ −3.4%, European sovereigns 15yr+ −6.3% to −9.4%) and US IG credit (−8.0%) were penalised by rising long rates and wider spreads in a risk-off environment. We intend to use the current pressure on long rates to modestly extend duration, as we view the risk of a sustained upward move as limited.

Conclusion

Financial markets are distinctive in that both opportunities and excesses originate from the same mechanisms of human psychology. Fear and greed are intrinsic to price formation — the challenge is to position accordingly, diversifying portfolios in the most rational and objective way possible. That is what we strive to do: identify dynamics, assess risks, and make informed decisions — not react to events.

This quarter, the forest showed us an American economy more resilient than feared despite successive shocks, private markets whose structural fragilities are beginning to surface, and a technological revolution that simultaneously creates and destroys value. In increasingly interconnected markets where investor behaviour remains fundamentally unchanged, it is this forest we seek to read — to decide whether to go around it, or which path to take through it.

Shadows are appearing, to be sure. Far from a negative message, we see in them a confirmation that the key to sound allocation lies in asset diversification and a deep understanding of the mechanisms that drive them — and that is precisely what our performance this quarter reflects.
